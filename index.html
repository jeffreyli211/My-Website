Who am I?

My name is Jeffrey Li. I'm a 21-year-old from New York City, and currently in my fourth and final year studying computer science at Boston University.

Why computer science?

Much of my passion for computer science sparked from my love of video games throughout my childhood. Growing up with two older brothers who constantly brought home the latest games to play from various different genres. From The Legend of Zelda: The Wind Waker, to Resident Evil 3: Nemesis, to Call of Duty 4: Modern Warfare, I quickly grew fond of the virtual worlds that all these different titles brought me into. Upon entering my first year of college back in 2017, this fascination I had with games led me to the curiosities of what made them tick. How do all the games that I fell in love with work?

This curiosity drove me to explore the field of computer science. In the process of learning the answers to my many questions about games and game development, I discovered a new passion in the form of programming. Every new programming project I took on felt like a new game I was playing, with a new type of puzzle to solve.

Triangle Rasterization

This project demonstrates the application of Bresenham's algorithm for line drawing on a two-dimensional canvas to rasterize lines and triangles. By specifying three vertices, the sides of the triangle are incrementally drawn, and the algorithm fills the lines of pixels between the sides. Smooth color shading was also implemented via bilinear interpolation to determine the color of each pixel in the triangle, based on the colors of its three endpoints. This was one of my first times being exposed to computer graphics and rendering techniques, and despite being a rather basic demonstration of shape-drawing, this rasterization technique has proven to be very fundamental within computer graphics, more specifically in the 3D rendering of triangle meshes to model objects.

3D Spider Modeling

Using the basic primitive shapes provided within Java's OpenGL library alongside the application of chained rotation transformations, this project focused on the creation of a 3D model creature, with multiple rotatable joints. Given the many moving parts of a spider, I found it to be a perfect reference model to showcase these concepts of 3D rotations and other types of transformations. Throughout this project, I was able to expose myself to many tasks that were new to me. For the first time, I manipulated simple 3D shapes to best fit the look of the spider I had envisioned in my mind. Every part of the model was created from scratch through transformations of a primitive shape, from the cyclinders with spherical end caps to form the leg components, to the spheres scaled to form the main body.
